How to Calculate Zakat

Zakat is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. It is an annual obligation to give 2.5% of your net wealth to those in need, provided your wealth exceeds the nisab threshold.

The nisab is the minimum amount of wealth that makes Zakat obligatory. It is equivalent to 85 grams of gold or 595 grams of silver. Most scholars recommend using the gold nisab. Your wealth must have been held for one full lunar year (hawl).

Add up all zakatable assets (cash, gold, silver, investments, business inventory), subtract short-term debts, and if the total exceeds nisab, pay 2.5% of the total.

Zakat is 2.5% of net zakatable assets held above the nisab threshold for one lunar year (hawl). Nisab is the minimum threshold — traditionally the value of 87.48g of gold or 612.36g of silver (scholars differ on which to apply). With gold at approximately $60/gram, nisab by gold standard ≈ $5,249. Silver nisab is lower, approximately $460 at current prices (verify current prices; metal values fluctuate).

Zakatable assets include: gold and silver (at market value), cash and bank deposits, trade goods (at market value), receivables expected to be paid, income from rented property. Non-zakatable assets include: primary residence, personal vehicle, household goods, tools used in work.

The hawl (one lunar year) begins when net zakatable assets first exceed nisab. If assets fall below nisab at any point during the year and then rise again, the hawl restarts from the second rise. If assets consistently stay above nisab, zakat is due at year-end on the total amount at that time.

Business inventory is zakatable at wholesale (cost) value for most scholars; finished goods at market value for others. Debts owed to you are zakatable if you expect repayment within the year. Business liabilities (debts you owe) can be deducted from zakatable assets before the 2.5% is applied.
