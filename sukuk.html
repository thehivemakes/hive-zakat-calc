Understanding Sukuk Returns

Sukuk are Islamic financial certificates similar to bonds but structured to comply with Sharia law. Instead of earning interest, Sukuk holders receive a share of profit generated by the underlying asset.

The yield to maturity accounts for both the periodic profit distributions and any capital gain or loss at maturity (difference between purchase price and face value).

Sukuk are Sharia-compliant certificates representing ownership in a physical asset or a share in a business venture, structured to generate returns without paying interest (riba). The most common structure, Ijara sukuk, is backed by a leased asset — the certificate holder receives periodic rent payments from the lessee, not interest.

Yield calculation: sukuk yield = (periodic income / purchase price) × frequency annualized. An Ijara sukuk purchased at $1,000 par paying $25 quarterly has an annual yield of ($25 × 4) / $1,000 = 10%. If purchased at a discount ($950), yield = $100 / $950 = 10.5%. This calculator follows the same yield-to-maturity conventions as conventional bonds, applied to the sukuk's income stream.

Key difference from bonds: sukuk holders are co-owners of underlying assets, not lenders. This means sukuk can theoretically lose value if the underlying asset loses value (conventional bonds repay face value at maturity regardless of asset value). In practice, sovereign and corporate sukuk often have buyback provisions maintaining capital protection.

Global sukuk market: estimated $800 billion outstanding as of 2024, with Malaysia, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Indonesia as major issuers. GCC sovereign sukuk are commonly used by Islamic pension funds and takaful operators as Sharia-compliant fixed-income alternatives.
