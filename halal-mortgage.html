Comparing Conventional and Islamic Home Financing

Islamic home financing avoids interest (riba) by structuring the transaction as a sale (Murabaha) or lease-to-own (Ijara). The total cost may be similar to conventional mortgages, but the structure is fundamentally different.

In Murabaha home financing, the bank buys the property and sells it to you at cost plus profit, payable in installments. The total price is fixed and does not compound. In Ijara, you lease the property with an option to purchase at the end.

The calculator compares total cost of ownership between conventional mortgage financing and the two primary Islamic home finance structures: Murabaha (cost-plus-profit, discussed on the Murabaha page) and Diminishing Musharaka (co-ownership with gradual buyout — the most common structure for UK and US Islamic mortgages).

Diminishing Musharaka works as follows: the bank and buyer co-own the property at purchase. The buyer pays monthly: (1) a usage fee (rent) for the bank's share, and (2) an installment that gradually buys out the bank's share. As the bank's ownership decreases, rent decreases accordingly. At the end of the term, the buyer owns 100%.

Example: $400,000 property, 20% down payment, remaining $320,000 co-owned with bank. Monthly payment covers bank's share rent plus buyout installment. Over a 30-year term at an equivalent rate to a 6% conventional mortgage, total payments may be comparable to the conventional total — the main difference is the legal structure, not necessarily the total cost.

Islamic mortgage products are available through specialist lenders (Guidance Residential in the US, Al Rayan Bank and HSBC Amanah in the UK). Availability and exact structures vary significantly by country. Consult a qualified Islamic finance advisor before committing to any product.
